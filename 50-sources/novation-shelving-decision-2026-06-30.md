# Novation shelving decision memo

## Summary

This memo converts the USD.AI and Nebius evidence into a project decision: the current-market GPU compute novation thesis is effectively falsified under the original LBH0 kill criteria. The right status is not permanently dead, but shelved with a tripwire for supply loosening or GPU price crashes.

## Durable Takeaways

- Current-market novation should be shelved because LBH0 - exit demand exists - failed cleanly.
- USD.AI and Nebius are independent, well-positioned sources against current seller-side offload demand.
- More calls asking whether anyone wants to offload committed capacity would be redundant invalidation, not rigor.
- The thesis can be revisited if supply catches up, next-gen GPU supply creates a glut, or GPU prices crash enough to revive over-commitment pressure.
- The falsification process only matters if clean pre-committed kill criteria actually stop work.

## Raw Context

Let me give you the straight read, because you've reached a real decision point and the worst thing I could do is hedge it into mush.

Where novation stands: effectively falsified for the current market, and you should believe it.

Not "looking shaky." Falsified, on the terms you set. Your own kill criteria from day one said LBH0 (exit demand exists) was a kill-switch. You now have two independent, maximally-well-positioned sources - a neocloud and a compute-lender whose entire book depends on seeing these contracts - both saying, unprompted and with a coherent why, that the demand isn't there. USD.AI said it three times in one call. The mechanism is clear and not hand-wavy: supply-constrained market means people are short, not long, so nobody offloads. That's exactly the clean, cheap kill your whole falsification apparatus was built to find.

The discipline point matters here: you built this process specifically so you'd be able to walk away fast when the evidence said to, instead of falling in love and grinding for months. This is that moment. If you don't act on a kill this clean, the whole pre-committed-criteria exercise was theater. The system worked - honor it.

The one honest asterisk: it's market-conditional, not dead forever. If supply catches up and the next-gen GPU glut crashes prices the way H100s crashed, the over-commitment overhang returns and this reanimates. So the correct status is "shelved, with a tripwire" - not "buried." You'd revisit if prices fall. But you do not keep pushing on it now.

Should you keep trying to invalidate novation? No - and this is important. You're past the point where more invalidation is useful. You have a clean two-source kill with a mechanism. A third "nobody wants out" call doesn't teach you anything new; it's just you flinching from the conclusion. Continuing to hunt for novation disconfirmation now would be procrastination disguised as rigor - the exact failure mode inverted. The evidence is in. Don't keep poking a thesis that's already given you its answer.
