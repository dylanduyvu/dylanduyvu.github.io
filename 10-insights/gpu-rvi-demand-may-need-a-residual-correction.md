# GPU RVI demand may need a residual correction

## Claim

GPU residual value insurance demand may be muted while operators believe residual values will stay extremely high. Bernie expects selling RVI to get easier after a GPU residual correction makes downside risk concrete.

## Why It Matters

This explains a sales-friction pattern: customers are curious, use RVI quotes as a pricing gut check, then do not buy because they think the guaranteed residual is too low. The willingness to pay may be cyclical and catalyzed by a visible market drawdown.

## Evidence

- 2026-06-16: Bernie said American Compute's biggest growth problem is customers using him as a gut check and not buying the insurance.
- 2026-06-16: He said customers often already have internal residual numbers and use American Compute as an external sanity check.
- 2026-06-16: He has offered appraisals, but they have not seemed popular.
- 2026-06-16: He said operators are overconfident, including customers expecting H100s to resell for 70% of original value after three years.
- 2026-06-16: Bernie said the current scarcity-driven market is not sustainable and that residuals will drop once supply comes online.
- 2026-06-16: He compared RVI sales after a correction to selling cybersecurity after an incident.

## Implications

- RVI demand may be a timing problem, not only a product problem.
- The product may need to target buyers already forced to recognize residual downside: lessors, insurers, banks, credit committees, distressed operators, or post-correction markets.
- A data product should quantify overconfidence and downside scenarios, not just report current high resale marks.
- Follow-up should ask for cases where quote/gut-check behavior turned into a purchase and what changed.

## Counterpoints / Uncertainty

- Barkr's call suggests some GPU clients already pay for guarantee-backed valuations, so demand is not absent across the market.
- American Compute's insurance wrapper may face different buyer friction than Barkr's warranty/guarantee wrapper.
- Operators may be right in the short term if scarcity persists longer than expected.
