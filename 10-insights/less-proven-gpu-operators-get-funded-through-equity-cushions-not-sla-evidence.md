# Less-proven GPU operators get funded through equity cushions, not SLA evidence

## Claim

When unknown or less-proven GPU operators get funded anyway, the observed workaround may be more borrower equity rather than better SLA or ops diligence. In Bernie's example, a $10M equipment deal can get financed if the operator brings $5M of cash. He later generalized the point: extra equity can resolve many kinds of lender concern.

## Why It Matters

This weakens the pure SLA-verification wedge. If the way lenders get comfortable with less-proven operators is to lower lender exposure through a large equity cushion, then the priced lever may be capital structure / LTV, not monitoring.

It also makes the equity example less diagnostic. The extra equity may not specifically compensate for missing SLA/operator transparency. It may simply be the generic way lenders solve almost any discomfort: reduce advance rate, increase borrower skin in the game, and make the lender safer.

It does not fully kill SLA as a problem. SLA evidence may still matter for repeat operators, insurance, covenants, or future financing. But it says the exception-case funding mechanism Bernie has seen is not "third-party proof of uptime"; it is "the operator has enough cash in the deal that the lender is safer."

## Evidence

- 2026-07-01: Dylan asked Bernie whether he had ever seen unknown / less-proven operators get funded anyway.
- 2026-07-01: Bernie replied, "Yes I have. They bring more equity to the deal. E.g. $10M of equipment, the operator brings $5M of cash themselves."
- 2026-07-01: Dylan asked whether extra equity is the blunt way to get comfortable when the operator is less proven, instead of diligencing future SLA delivery directly.
- 2026-07-01: Bernie replied that generally, extra equity can resolve any kind of lender concern.

## Implications

- Treat "SLA dashboard for lenders" as further weakened unless another lender says verified performance changes approval or terms.
- Reframe the unknown-operator financing question around what substitutes for reputation: equity, collateral coverage, stronger offtake, guarantees, insurance, or structure.
- Test whether SLA/ops diligence can reduce the required equity cushion. That is sharper than asking whether lenders generally care about uptime.
- The first viable wedge may be evidence or insurance that changes LTV / equity requirement, not standalone monitoring.
- Do not overread equity cushions as proof that SLA opacity is the binding issue. They may be a generic cure for credit, execution, collateral, concentration, or operator concerns.
- Direct lender tests should ask what they would do at the same equity level with and without better operator/SLA evidence.

## Counterpoints / Uncertainty

- This is one example from Bernie, not a full market map.
- Bernie may see RVI / equipment-finance-adjacent cases more than SLA-specific lender workflows.
- Equity cushions and SLA evidence could be complements: a lender might still require both for smaller or weaker operators.
- USD.AI's original pain remains live because Harry specifically tied SLA assurance to pricing more aggressively.
- Extra equity solving "any kind" of concern does not tell us whether a specialized diligence product can reduce required equity in marginal cases.
